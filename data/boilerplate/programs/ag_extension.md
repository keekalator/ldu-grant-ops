# Northern California Land Development — Program Description Boilerplate

## Vision
The Kikas — a mother-daughter duo — are creative urban planners and developers expanding their real estate portfolio with community at the core of every movement. For this vision, they are further solidifying their partnership with Weleh Dennis — co-founder and lead creative of Kids of Immigrants (a 10-year-old apparel brand) and a Sacramento native — on the land acquisition, development, design, and programming. It is a family expansion that brings together all of their minds and gifts to create something truly unique. They are a cooperative building in LA to be an example of industry, and this land development extends their joined efforts into a new frontier.

The Northern California land acquisition is its own vision — connected to the Crenshaw campus through shared mission, but distinct in execution and ambition. Twenty to eighty acres in the Sacramento Valley agricultural corridor where nature and industry connect.

## Why This Matters
In 1910, Black farmers owned 16 million acres of land. Over the next century, more than 12 million acres were taken through systemic racism and discrimination. Today, only 1.4% of U.S. farmers are Black. An estimated $326 billion or more in land value has been lost.

The collective goal is to create and protect a legacy based on intergenerational healing. This endeavor matters not just for their family, but for every generation they can impact by connecting them to the land — to experiences, practices, crafts, and skillsets that cannot be learned in a city environment.

## Design Philosophy
Weleh Dennis leads the design philosophy and will serve as a land-based facilitator alongside The Kikas. His vision is rooted in the commitment to building in sync with nature — creating a living environment and example of sustainability practices. The level of execution they aspire to is exemplified by Tierra Patagonia, Camp Woodward, and Terunobu Fujimori's La Collina — references that demonstrate the depth of commitment to creating structures and environments that exist as part of the land, not apart from it.

## Projected Programming (Requires Funding)
- Seasonal exchange residencies (8-12 weeks) aligned with growing seasons
- Workforce development and trade certification: carpentry, woodworking, welding, farm structure construction
- Expansion of Studio WELEH's art practice: a barn housing the studio with land-based classes for art and craftsmanship, open seasonally and as a residency to youth and practicing artists
- Expanded makers lab with industrial sewing machines, CNC and fabrication tools for set design, presentation design, and skillshare — practices too large for the LA campus conducted on the land
- Outdoor action sports and physical development: skateboarding, BMX, parkour — participants learn to design, construct, and maintain the terrain they ride
- Wellness programming and facilities for both youth and elders
- Retreat and renewal programming for leadership development and creative residencies
- Homesteading
- Manufacturing facility expanding scalable fabrication capabilities between Los Angeles and Northern California

[NOTE: All programming is projected and requires land acquisition and development funding. Do not describe as active programs in applications.]

## Land Acquisition Strategy
Phase 1: Secure funding for land acquisition and architectural renderings for the development plan.

Phase 2: Begin development on the land — establish basic residential capacity, install water infrastructure, begin soil preparation, construct initial structures in the sustainability-focused vernacular. Participants contribute to construction as part of their training.

Phase 3: Expand to 60-80 acres with additional growing zones, full action sports facilities, Studio WELEH barn and expanded makers lab, manufacturing facility, wellness facilities, greenhouse/nursery, and retreat lodging.

## Grant Positioning
This is a first-time land acquisition by a family-owned cooperative enterprise with a proven track record of acquiring and developing community property in South Los Angeles. The cooperative includes The Kikas (experienced business owners who transformed a predatory check-cashing facility into a state-of-the-art creative campus) and Weleh Dennis (co-founder and lead creative of Kids of Immigrants, Sacramento native, and lead design facilitator for the land development).

The vision is rooted in the revolutionary act of Black land ownership and addresses multiple federal and state funding priorities: beginning farmer support, first-time land acquisition, workforce development, sustainable development, youth outdoor education, and rural economic development.
