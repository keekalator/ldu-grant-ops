## The Kikas — Founding Story
We are Kika Keith and Kika Howze — a mother and daughter who have been building together for as long as we can remember. Four generations of entrepreneurship, activism, and community building run through this family. Big Kika's grandfather owned the first Black-owned hotel and bar in Lancaster, Pennsylvania. Her mother was a Black Panther. Her parents traveled the country as freedom fighters. That legacy is not abstract to us — it is the reason we build the way we build.

Big Kika founded Sweet Strings, an inner-city youth orchestra with 300 youth enrolled and 1,000 more on the waiting list. She created Gorilla Life, a chlorophyll beverage line that reached over 300 Whole Foods stores nationwide. Kika Howze left Los Angeles at 17, spent over a decade in New York working in talent management, advertising, and multicultural marketing with brands like Coca-Cola, Google, Nike, and HBO — then came home to build alongside her mother.

LDU is the next chapter. We acquired a former predatory check-cashing facility on Crenshaw Boulevard and completely renovated it into a creative campus for family wellness, resources, and education. This is what we do: we take what was built to extract from our community and we transform it into something that develops the people who live here.

## Big Kika's Voice
"Every business I've built carries a piece of the same legacy: ownership with purpose, enterprise with integrity, and a deep belief that when we create, we create not just for ourselves, but for the generations coming after us. I want young people in our community to have access to imagination and infrastructure. I want them to see what's possible when you refuse to give up."

"When the system won't build with you, you build your own."

## Women Entrepreneur Narrative
Kika Keith and Kika Howze — "The Kikas" — are a mother-daughter duo who built Life Development University the same way their family has built everything: from the ground up, with their own hands, driven by purpose and spirit.

Big Kika was raised in Leimert Park. She grew up watching her family create institutions where none existed — her grandfather's hotel, her mother's activism, her parents' freedom work across the country. She carried that into her own life: an inner-city youth orchestra with 300 youth enrolled and a thousand more on the waiting list, a wellness beverage company that scaled to over 300 Whole Foods stores, and a for-profit enterprise on the very corridor where LDU now stands. The Crenshaw community has supported this family long before they were business owners — the trust is deep, the relationships are real, and the commitment runs both ways.

Kika Howze brought a different kind of education home. Fifteen years in New York City across music, advertising, film, and multicultural marketing — rooms with Coca-Cola, Google, Nike, HBO — taught her how institutions scale, how brands communicate, and how creative direction becomes economic infrastructure. She returned to Los Angeles and applied all of it to the family vision.

Together, they acquired a building on Crenshaw Boulevard, renovated it from a predatory check-cashing facility into a state-of-the-art creative campus, and founded Life Development University as a permanent commitment to workforce development, education, cooperative economics, and wellness. In its first quarter, the campus welcomed over 1,000 community members. They are not building a program. They are building an institution — and they are building it to last.

## Land Acquisition & Development Vision
The Kikas are creative urban planners and developers expanding their real estate portfolio with community at the core of every movement. For this vision, they are further solidifying their partnership with Weleh Dennis — co-founder and lead creative of Kids of Immigrants (a 10-year-old apparel brand) and a Sacramento native — on the land acquisition, development, design, and programming. It is a family expansion that brings together all of their minds and gifts to create something truly unique. They are a cooperative building in LA to be an example of industry, and this land development extends their joined efforts into a new frontier.

The vision for Northern California is its own execution — twenty to eighty acres of land in the Sacramento Valley agricultural corridor where nature and industry connect.

This vision is rooted in something much larger than real estate. In 1910, Black farmers owned 16 million acres of land. Over the next century, more than 12 million acres were taken through systemic racism and discrimination. Today, only 1.4% of U.S. farmers are Black. An estimated $326 billion or more in land value has been lost. The collective goal is to create and protect a legacy based on intergenerational healing. This endeavor matters not just for their family, but for every generation they can impact by connecting them to the land — to experiences, practices, crafts, and skillsets that cannot be learned in a city environment.

The land will house seasonal exchange residencies, workforce development and trade certification programs, the expansion of Studio WELEH's art practice with land-based classes for art and craftsmanship, outdoor action sports and physical development, wellness programming and facilities for both youth and elders, retreat and renewal programming, homesteading, and a manufacturing facility that expands scalable fabrication capabilities between Los Angeles and Northern California.

Weleh Dennis leads the design philosophy and will serve as a land-based facilitator alongside The Kikas. His vision draws from the commitment to building in sync with nature — creating a living environment and example of sustainability practices at the level of execution exemplified by Tierra Patagonia, Camp Woodward, and Terunobu Fujimori's La Collina.

The land is part of the family's constellation — connected to the Crenshaw campus through shared mission and shared people, but distinct in its vision and execution. This is about expanding their footprint beyond South Central and creating a framework where community members move between urban and rural environments for education, work, and renewal.

The model addresses multiple federal and state priorities: beginning farmer support, first-time land acquisition, workforce development, sustainable development, and youth outdoor education. The founders — alongside Weleh Dennis, a Sacramento native and experienced business owner — bring the same operational discipline that built a successful business and a nonprofit campus on Crenshaw Boulevard to this next phase of development.
