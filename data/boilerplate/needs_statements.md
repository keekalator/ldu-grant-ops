# LDU — Needs Statements Boilerplate

## Service Area Overview

Life Development University serves zip codes 90008 and 90043 in South Los Angeles — neighborhoods that include Baldwin Hills, View Park, Windsor Hills, Ladera Heights, Hyde Park, and Leimert Park. These are communities with deep cultural roots, intergenerational homeownership, and an enduring tradition of Black enterprise. They are also communities where structural underinvestment has left residents without the resources, programming, and infrastructure that comparable neighborhoods elsewhere in the city have long taken for granted.

LDU operates as a family resource center offering programming that spans all ages. Through strategic community partnerships with organizations that serve specific population groups — youth, elders, re-entry, families, artists, and small business owners — LDU is able to provide full-spectrum programming without absorbing the full cost of each program category. Partners deliver services through the campus; LDU provides the facility, the coordination, and the community trust. This model allows the campus to run as a self-sustaining venue while still making meaningful programming available to South Central families across every stage of life.

The populations LDU serves are the populations that opportunity zones were designed to reach: formerly incarcerated individuals, single parents, transitional workers, low-income community members, the chronically unemployed, and families navigating overlapping barriers to economic stability.

### Key Takeaways for Grant Applications
Unemployment in LDU's immediate service area runs between 8% and 10.8% — roughly double the national average. More than half the population relies on public health coverage. Labor force participation lags behind both state and national benchmarks. These are not communities lacking ambition or work ethic. They are communities where the infrastructure for economic advancement — training, technology, creative workspace, and business development resources — has never been built at the scale needed.

## What the Community Is Asking For

During LDU's first quarter of activation, the campus welcomed over 1,000 community members. Through direct engagement, data capture, and ongoing conversation, LDU has heard consistent requests for the following:

- Record expungement services (clearing past convictions to access employment, housing, and education)
- Art, food, and cultural events that reflect and celebrate the community
- A third space to work and create — somewhere between home and a corporate office
- A place to launch new ideas, test concepts, and build businesses
- Workshops and masterclasses led by working professionals
- Film and content screenings
- Women's wellness circles and support groups
- A meet-up hub for launching outdoor classes and clubs (run clubs, walk clubs, yoga, sound healing)
- Education and vocational tools for career development
- Job placement and employment pipeline access

Every one of these needs currently requires community members to leave the Crenshaw corridor entirely. Residents travel downtown, to the Westside, or out of state to access the programming, creative infrastructure, and professional development that LDU is building on their own block.

## Barriers Facing the Community

The barriers are not singular. They overlap, compound, and reinforce each other:

- Employment access: Unemployment at 2x the national average with limited local training infrastructure
- Education gaps: High school completion below national benchmarks; limited post-secondary pathways nearby
- Affordable creative space: No accessible creative workspace between downtown and the Westside
- Technology access: Significant digital divide (see Technology section below)
- Wellness infrastructure: Virtually nonexistent on the corridor outside of retail-driven businesses
- Re-entry barriers: Past convictions blocking access to jobs, housing, and capital
- Business formalization: Small business owners and creatives lacking the administrative skills (financial literacy, project management, LLC formation) to scale their ideas

### Local Workforce Gaps
The Los Angeles hospitality, customer service, and entertainment sectors consistently face hiring challenges — not for lack of willing workers, but for lack of qualified, trained candidates from the immediate community. Employers in these industries need workers with customer service training, point-of-sale experience, inventory management skills, and the professional development foundations that LDU's programming is designed to deliver. The emerging AI economy compounds this gap: as employers across every sector integrate artificial intelligence into their operations, workers without basic digital and AI literacy risk permanent exclusion from the labor market.

### The Ownership Lab: A Cooperative Model for Workforce Training
Adjacent to LDU, The Kikas operate a successful for-profit retail business on Crenshaw Boulevard. Their long-term vision is converting this operation into The Ownership Lab — a cooperative equity retail incubator where employees learn and earn entrepreneurship through the business itself. This co-op model, developed by Kika Keith and managed alongside Sheila, demonstrates the founders' depth of operational expertise and their commitment to creating replicable pathways from employment to ownership. The Ownership Lab embodies LDU's core philosophy: workforce development is not just about jobs — it is about building the infrastructure for community members to own and operate businesses themselves.

The model draws on the franchise university concept: structured training tracks that take participants from entry-level skills through management development to full operator preparation. Life Development University provides the classroom instruction; The Ownership Lab provides real-world application. The three training tracks — Entry-Level Employee Certification, Management Development, and CEO/Operator Preparation — produce workers who are not just job-ready but ownership-ready.

This cooperative model has institutional backing: curriculum developed in partnership with USC's Iovine and Andre Young Academy and Los Angeles Southwest College, registered apprenticeship status pursued through WestCal Academy, and alignment with the County of Los Angeles Office of Cannabis Management's emerging workforce programs. Kika Keith has served as a senior consultant for the County's Office of Cannabis Management for eighteen months, directly informing the County's program development — positioning The Ownership Lab as a preferred workforce training pipeline.

## Arts Access Need

### The Current State of Arts Education
LAUSD has faced years of arts education budget cuts. The district's Arts Education branch has been cut by over 70%, and hundreds of elementary arts teachers have received reduction-in-force notices. A 2025 lawsuit against LAUSD alleged that the district misused tens of millions in Proposition 28 arts education funds — replacing existing arts teacher funding rather than supplementing it as the law requires. Parents and students in South LA have reported limited or no access to dance, theater, music, and visual arts instruction despite state funding mandates.

For a young person on the Crenshaw corridor who wants to pursue a creative career, the options are bleak. There are no professional-grade creative training facilities in the immediate neighborhood. Students must travel downtown, to the Westside, or leave the state entirely to access the kind of hands-on instruction, mentorship, and studio space that Studio WELEH and LDU provide.

### What Studio WELEH Solves
Studio WELEH brings hands-on workshops led by industry leaders who have applied their expertise and methodology in their own creative careers. Weleh Dennis — co-founder of Kids of Immigrants, an Inglewood-based artist with exhibitions at Art Basel and costume design for Coachella — offers the kind of practitioner-led instruction that does not exist in local schools or community centers. The programming connects high school and college-aged community members directly to working professionals, bridging the gap between aspiration and professional practice.

Ben Caldwell's KAOS Network in Leimert Park — a community arts center offering digital arts, media arts, and multimedia training since 1984 — demonstrates both the community's hunger for creative technology programming and the historical precedent for culture-based institutions on this corridor. LDU and Studio WELEH build on that legacy while expanding the scope to include apparel, fabrication, large-scale art, and the land-based creative programming that the Northern California vision will make possible.

## Technology and AI Gap

### The Digital Divide on Crenshaw
The Crenshaw corridor has been the focus of multiple digital equity initiatives precisely because it has been historically disconnected from basic broadband infrastructure. Destination Crenshaw's Digital Equity Zone Initiative, supported by Cisco, the California Community Foundation, and Digital Equity LA, launched a free community Wi-Fi pilot in late 2025 because the core of the Crenshaw corridor — the highest concentration of Black-owned residential and commercial property in the area — had never been connected to any form of broadband. The Crenshaw Community Connectivity Pilot installed 1.5 miles of underground fiber from Leimert Park Plaza to the Hyde Park Metro Station as a first step toward closing a gap that has persisted for decades.

Separately, SoLa Impact received $1 million in federal funding to build a Tech and Entertainment Center at the Crenshaw Lofts development, focused on bridging the racial digital divide for South LA youth. These investments confirm a documented reality: the community surrounding LDU has been structurally excluded from digital infrastructure and the economic opportunities that connectivity enables.

### LDU's AI Lab Vision
LDU's AI lab (in development) addresses this gap at the practical level:
- Entry-level AI skills development for daily personal and professional use
- Practical application training for small businesses and creatives showing how to use AI tools to accelerate their operations
- Pipeline development connecting trained community members to data and AI companies seeking entry-level skilled workers
- Digital literacy as a workforce foundation — ensuring the community is not left behind as every industry integrates artificial intelligence

This is not a theoretical program. It is a direct response to the reality that the neighborhoods surrounding LDU were only connected to public Wi-Fi in late 2025, and that the skills gap between this community and the digital economy grows wider every year.

## Wellness Need

### What Exists on the Corridor
Wellness infrastructure on the Crenshaw corridor is virtually nonexistent. St. John's Community Center operates but has significant difficulty attracting participants from the community beyond food drives. Everything else is retail-driven — shops and services, not programming.

Three notable exceptions have expressed interest in partnering with LDU: Nappily Naturals, Suprmrkt, and Crenshaw Yoga. Each offers wellness-adjacent services but lacks the facility, programming infrastructure, and community reach to deliver at scale. Crenshaw Yoga in particular is extremely underutilized by the community — an indicator not of lack of need, but of the absence of a central hub that connects residents to the resources that already exist.

### LDU's Wellness Model
LDU addresses mental health, nutrition, fitness, elder care, and youth development — not as a standalone wellness provider, but as the hub that connects community members to vetted wellness partners. Through the data capture process, LDU informs residents of wellness offerings as they become available: private and group therapy, music and sound healing, yoga, healing circles, motherhood courses and support groups, run clubs, walk clubs, and more.

Wellness at LDU is both integrated into broader programming (every workshop, activation, and event is designed with the whole person in mind) and offered as its own dedicated track through community partners. LDU is selective about who it brings in as partners — each must share the ethos of holistic community wellness as a core value, not a marketing message.

## The Crenshaw Corridor: Development Pressure and LDU's Role

### What Has Changed
The Crenshaw corridor has undergone significant transformation in the last five years. The Metro K Line brought new transit infrastructure but also new development pressure. Destination Crenshaw is building a 1.3-mile open-air museum celebrating Black culture and history. SoFi Stadium has made the broader area a destination for major events. New housing developments, coffee shops, restaurants, and retail are appearing along the corridor — but the overwhelming majority of this development is not designed for or by the people who have lived here for generations.

### What LDU Fills That No One Else Is
LDU's role on the corridor is fundamentally about property acquisition and economic self-determination. The founders are breaking the cycle that defines urban development: without land ownership, communities have no power over what gets built and who benefits. LDU is opening the model for community-owned development on the corridor and expanding that model through the acquisition of neighboring properties to become a catalyst for small business economic development — the heart of any thriving community.

The Kikas are actively engaging with Black business owners from across the community to expand their operations to the corridor. This is cooperative economics in practice — directly inspired by Theaster Gates' creative urban development model and Marcus Garvey's principle of cooperative economics. LDU is not waiting for development to happen to this community. It is building the development from within.

## Northern California: Addressing Regional Need

### Yolo County Context
Yolo County (population approximately 230,000) is anchored by agriculture as its leading industry, with Woodland and Winters serving as the primary communities in the northern part of the county. The region faces its own workforce challenges: agriculture remains the dominant employer, but the workforce is aging and the county's high school dropout rate has historically been concentrated in Woodland. Despite proximity to UC Davis, there is limited vocational training infrastructure connecting rural youth to trades, fabrication, and the creative economy.

### What LDU's Land Development Addresses
The Northern California land acquisition serves both LA community members traveling to the land for seasonal residencies and immersive programming, and Sacramento Valley youth and residents who would benefit from the vocational, arts, and wellness programming that the campus will provide. The vision creates a framework where urban and rural communities strengthen each other — LA provides the industry connections and creative infrastructure; the land provides the space, the agricultural knowledge, and the environment for practices that cannot happen in a city setting.

The land development also addresses federal and state priorities for beginning farmer support and agricultural workforce development in a region where the agricultural workforce is contracting and diversification of the rural economy is a stated policy goal.

## Continuum of Care: LDU's Wraparound Model

LDU has built a network of intentional community partners who provide wraparound services addressing the whole person — not just vocational skills:
- Permanent and transitional housing assistance
- Court Appointed Special Advocate (CASA) services
- One-on-one life coaching sessions
- Expungement and re-entry services
- Childcare support
- Mental health and wellness resources

This collaborative model reduces attrition, increases engagement, improves academic and career outcomes, and builds long-term community resilience. Community partners are an essential part of every participant's support system, offering services that address personal and familial needs so individuals can fully engage in their educational and career development.

## Pathways Framework

LDU's programming is structured around two complementary pathways:

### Pathway to Self-Sufficiency (Workforce Development)
A progression from workshops and job-readiness through retail experience, foundation and entry-level training, management development, apprenticeship, internship, and certificate programs. Participants gain industry-recognized certifications, skills training, career counseling, and professional development in high-demand industries.

### Pathway to Self-Determination (Life Skills & Wellness)
Five pillars supporting the whole person: personal planning with a life coach, legal services (pro-bono and low-bono), expungement resources, life skills (technology, communication, custom success planning), financial literacy (budgeting, credit building, business formalization), and holistic wellness (mental health, creative outlets, physical fitness).
